One Bar, Full Body: Calisthenics Training at Home

You don't need a fully equipped gym to train your whole body. With one pull-up bar, you can build pull, push, core, and full-body control — right from your living room.

A pull-up bar is probably the most underrated piece of training equipment you can own. It takes up no space, costs a fraction of a gym membership, and yet it holds almost everything you need to train your entire body — if you know how to use it. This isn't about collecting more machines. It's about getting better at controlling your own body with what's already hanging in your doorway or bolted to your wall.

Why One Bar Is Enough

At Pole Peak, we rarely think of training as something you do to get "stronger" for its own sake. We think of it as control — the ability to move your body precisely where you want it, at the pace you choose. A pull-up bar is one of the best tools for building exactly that kind of control, because it forces you to work with your own bodyweight, your own grip, and your own balance. There's no machine guiding the movement for you. It's just you, the bar, and how much command you have over yourself along the way.

Setting Up Your Bar

Most home setups fall into three categories: a doorframe bar that wedges into an opening, a wall-mounted bar, or a freestanding rig like the ones from Gornation or similar brands, which often come with multiple grip positions and sometimes built-in dip handles. All three work fine for the exercises below — pick whichever fits your home and your budget. Whichever you choose, always check the weight rating and test the bar with controlled weight before committing fully to a movement. A freestanding rig requires no drilling into the wall, which makes it an easy choice if you're renting.

Find Your Starting Point

Below are 20 exercises split across five categories: pull, push, core, legs/hips, and full-body control. Each one is tagged with a level, so you can quickly find the ones that fit where you are right now. Use the filters to find your starting point — and come back once you're ready for the next level.

Pull

Beginner

Scapula Pulls

Hang passively from the bar, then pull your shoulder blades down and together without bending your elbows. Builds the control every pull-up starts with.

Beginner

Controlled Negatives

Jump or step yourself up to the top position, then lower yourself as slowly as possible all the way down. Build strength in the phase you'll later pull yourself through.

Intermediate

Strict Pull-Ups

From a full dead hang to chin over the bar, no kipping or swinging. Quality over quantity — every rep should look the same.

Advanced

Archer Pull-Ups

Pull with one arm as the primary driver while the other stays extended out to the side. Builds one-sided control on the way toward a one-arm pull-up.

Push

Beginner

Elevated Push-Ups

Place your hands on your rig's lower bar or a stable edge and do push-ups with your torso raised. A solid entry point if you don't have dip handles yet.

Beginner

Negative Dips

If your bar has dip handles, start at the top and lower yourself under control as far as your shoulders comfortably allow. Build confidence at the bottom of the movement.

Intermediate

Strict Dips

Full range down and up on the dip handles, controlled the whole way. Keep your shoulders away from your ears and avoid swinging your legs.

Advanced

Korean Dips

A dip with a horizontal extension at the top, where your body travels forward and up. Requires solid shoulder and core control.

Core

Beginner

Hanging Knee Raises

Hang from the bar and lift your knees toward your chest without swinging. Focus on using your abs, not momentum.

Intermediate

Hanging Leg Raises

The same movement as knee raises, but with straight legs. Lift slowly, and lower even more slowly.

Advanced

Toes to Bar

Lift straight legs all the way up to the bar. Requires hamstring and hip mobility just as much as raw ab strength.

Advanced

Hanging Windshield Wipers

With your legs raised, swing them under control from side to side like a windshield wiper. Pure rotational control through the core.

Legs & Hips

Beginner

Assisted Pistol Squats

Hold your rig's low bar for balance and lower yourself on one leg as far as you can control. Build depth gradually over time.

Intermediate

Hanging L-Sit Hold

Hang from the bar and raise straight legs to a perpendicular L-position. Hold it still — it's the hold time that builds the control.

Intermediate

Single-Leg Hanging Raises

Lift one straight leg at a time toward the bar while the other stays still. Quickly reveals asymmetries between sides.

Advanced

Full Pistol Squat

The same movement as the assisted version, but without any support from the bar. Pure leg and balance control from bottom to top.

Full-Body Control

Beginner

German Hang

Hang with straight arms and shoulders rotated back, body hanging below the bar. Opens the shoulders and back — and is the foundation for several more advanced skills.

Intermediate

Skin the Cat

From a hang, roll your body backward and through your arms into an inverted hang, then reverse it. One of the most satisfying skills to master — pure, flowing control.

Advanced

Tuck Front Lever Hold

From a hang, pull your knees to your chest and raise your body to horizontal, back facing the floor. Hold time over reps.

Advanced

Back Lever (Tuck)

From a German hang, roll your body to a horizontal position facing the floor, knees tucked in. Requires stillness through your whole body to hold steady.

Putting It Together as a Session

You don't need to do all 20 exercises in one go. Pick 5-6 from different categories and build a session that matches where you are right now:

  • Beginner flow: Scapula Pulls, Controlled Negatives, Elevated Push-Ups, Hanging Knee Raises, Assisted Pistol Squats, and German Hang. 3 rounds, control over high reps.
  • Intermediate circuit: Strict Pull-Ups, Strict Dips, Hanging Leg Raises, Hanging L-Sit Hold, and Skin the Cat. 4 rounds with full focus on clean execution.
  • Advanced control circuit: Archer Pull-Ups, Korean Dips, Toes to Bar, Full Pistol Squat, and Tuck Front Lever Hold. 3-4 rounds, shorter rest, hold time built into every round.

The goal is never just to grind out more reps. It's to be able to stop the movement wherever you want — and start it again exactly when you choose.

From Bar to Pole

It's no coincidence that so many of the exercises above — German Hang, Skin the Cat, the front and back lever holds — are also foundations of pole dance and aerial work. The control you build hanging from a bar at home is exactly the same control that later lets you move freely and precisely on a pole: knowing where your body is in space, and being able to stop or change direction exactly when you want to.

That's why calisthenics is a core part of what Pole Peak Copenhagen is built around. Not as a goal in itself, but as the foundation that makes everything else possible.
